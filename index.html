Aleron was built because we couldn’t find it.

Jason Yim

Patient Number One

I started Aleron because I have watched two things happen. I have seen young people die early from diseases we file under old age, heart disease, cancer, stroke, people with families and plans and decades they assumed they had, most of whom believed they were healthy. I have also seen people in their 70s surfing, building companies, traveling with their grandchildren, fully present and fully alive. Both groups might be outliers, but I know which side I want to be on, and I will do everything I can to stack the deck in my favor. Then it got personal. A clinical-grade genetic test came back with a pathogenic ATM variant my doctor had never tested for. I ran my full picture through the system we built and it surfaced ten clinically significant findings. My physician, through no fault of his own, would have caught three or four. The rest, the ones that point toward an early death, would have stayed invisible. Clarity is not comfortable, but I cannot be a passenger in my own life.

Aleron is the system that produces that clarity. Four sources of data actually matter: your genome, your blood, your wearables, and your history. Each one alone tells a partial story, like the blind men each touching one part of the elephant and arguing over the whole animal. The truth lives in the connections between them, and reading all of it at once is a superhuman task. A physician holds roughly 150 units of information in working memory, brilliant but bringing a knife to a data gunfight. So we built an AI medical board, four frontier reasoning models orchestrated in a truth-maximizing debate over your data, borrowing the structure of tumor boards and intelligence review. The board maps the entire action space across your disease risks and ranks the few moves that matter most right now. Then your physician, the one person who knows your name and your data, sits over the whole thing as judge and delivers it to you personally. The product is clarity. All of your data, every relevant study, one thing to do this week. And because each move you make pulls a new part of the picture into focus, the board reconvenes and the work compounds. Your life is a movie, not a snapshot.
